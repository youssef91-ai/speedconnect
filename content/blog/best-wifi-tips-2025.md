Most people blame their ISP when their internet feels slow — but in the majority of cases, the bottleneck is in the home network itself, not the connection coming into the house.

**1. Position your router for maximum coverage.** Place it centrally in your home, elevated off the floor, away from walls and metal surfaces. Router antennas broadcast in a sphere, not a cone — height matters.

**2. Switch to the 5 GHz band for nearby devices.** If you're within 10 metres of your router, 5 GHz offers significantly higher throughput than 2.4 GHz. Most modern devices connect automatically to the faster band when available.

**3. Change your Wi-Fi channel.** Your neighbours' routers may be flooding channels 1, 6, and 11. Use an app like WiFi Analyzer (Android) or Wireless Diagnostics (macOS) to find the emptiest channel and set it manually.

**4. Enable 802.11ax (Wi-Fi 6) if your router supports it.** Wi-Fi 6 is up to 40% faster than Wi-Fi 5 and handles congestion much better in dense environments.

**5. Update your router firmware.** Manufacturers release updates that improve stability, security, and performance. Check your router admin panel monthly.

**6. Reboot your router weekly.** Scheduled reboots prevent memory leaks and stale connection tables from degrading performance over time.

**7. Reduce interference from other devices.** Microwaves, baby monitors, and some smart home devices operate on 2.4 GHz. Keep your router away from these and switch affected devices to 5 GHz or Ethernet.

**8. Use QoS to prioritise video calls and streaming.** Most routers let you prioritise traffic types. Set video conferencing and streaming above cloud backup and downloads.

**9. Consider powerline adapters for difficult rooms.** If a room has thick walls or is far from your router, a powerline adapter uses your home's electrical wiring to deliver a wired-equivalent connection without running cables.

**10. Upgrade your router if it's over 4 years old.** Wi-Fi technology has improved dramatically. An older router may be the ceiling on your entire home network's performance, regardless of your ISP plan.
