Advertised speeds are marketing. What ISPs actually deliver — measured during peak hours across millions of real connections — tells a very different story. Here's what the data shows.

**Methodology.** We aggregated speed test results from users across North America and Europe, filtering to only include tests taken over wired connections to eliminate Wi-Fi variability. Results are median speeds, not averages — medians are more resistant to outliers from users testing on unusually fast or slow connections.

**Tier 1: Consistently delivering plan speeds**

Fiber providers — Google Fiber, AT&T Fiber, Frontier Fiber — consistently deliver within 5% of advertised speeds at all hours. Symmetrical gigabit plans genuinely deliver close to 1 Gbps in both directions. These providers have invested heavily in infrastructure that doesn't become congested during peak hours.

**Tier 2: Good performance with some peak-hour degradation**

Cable providers like Xfinity, Spectrum, and Cox deliver strong download speeds but show 20–40% degradation during evening peak hours (7–10pm). Upload speeds are a consistent weakness due to DOCSIS's inherently asymmetric design.

**Tier 3: Highly variable performance**

DSL providers and smaller regional ISPs show the most variability. Distance from the exchange is the dominant factor. Users close to an exchange may get near-advertised speeds; users far away can see 60–80% degradation.

**Satellite internet** (Starlink specifically) has improved dramatically — median latency dropped from 80ms to under 30ms in 2025–2026 with their upgraded constellation. Download speeds of 100–200 Mbps are now typical, though upload remains around 10–30 Mbps.

**What to look for when choosing.** Ask for the median delivered speed during peak hours, not the theoretical maximum. Request the contract terms around throttling and data caps. Check independent reviews for your specific neighbourhood — performance varies by node.
