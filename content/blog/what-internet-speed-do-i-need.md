ISPs will happily sell you a gigabit plan when 50 Mbps would meet your needs comfortably. But underestimating your needs leaves everyone frustrated. Here's how to calculate what you actually need.

**The baseline per activity:**

- Standard definition video streaming: 3–5 Mbps
- HD video streaming (1080p): 5–10 Mbps
- 4K video streaming: 15–25 Mbps
- Video calls (one person): 3–5 Mbps upload + download
- Online gaming: 3–6 Mbps, but ping matters far more than bandwidth
- Remote work with video calls: 10–25 Mbps per person
- Smart home devices: 1–5 Mbps total for most setups
- Cloud gaming (GeForce NOW, Xbox Cloud): 15–35 Mbps

**How to calculate your household needs:**

Count simultaneous users and activities at peak time. A family of four with two people on video calls, one streaming 4K, and one gaming simultaneously needs approximately: 10 + 10 + 25 + 5 = 50 Mbps, with a safety buffer making 75–100 Mbps a comfortable choice.

**Upload is increasingly important.** Remote work, video calls, cloud backup, and livestreaming all require substantial upload bandwidth. If anyone works from home regularly, prioritise a plan with at least 20–50 Mbps upload — which typically means fiber.

**Latency matters as much as bandwidth for many uses.** A 50 Mbps fiber connection with 10ms ping will provide a better gaming and video call experience than a 500 Mbps cable connection with 40ms ping and high jitter.

**When to upgrade to gigabit.** If your household has 5+ people, multiple 4K streams, active cloud backup, and at least one person working from home full-time, gigabit plans often make sense economically — the per-Mbps cost at gigabit tier is often lower than mid-tier plans.

**When you don't need gigabit.** If you live alone or with one other person and primarily browse, stream, and occasionally video call, 100–200 Mbps is more than sufficient. The marginal benefit of gigabit for light users is essentially zero.
